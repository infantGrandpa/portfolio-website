The Goal

Devise a scavenger hunt style game for groups to explore their community while engaging in fun activities with friends.

Research & Early Designs

Competitive Analysis

Actionbound

  • Users share custom scavenger hunts with the community
  • Small community limits full potential

Let's Roam

  • Professionally crafted scavenger hunts ensure quality
  • Heavy up-front fee increases barrier to entry

Pokémon Go

  • Procedurally generated content provides endless hunts
  • Little to connect different stops to each other

From this analysis, I determined we would need a mix of procedurally generated hunts to create a wealth of content but allow users to craft and share their own for better quality experiences.

User Research

My next course of action was to interview users. I interviewed three people within the age range of 18-35. Once again, I walked away with several key takeaways:

  • Scavenger hunts are not a regular activity in people's lives.
  • Competition can be a key draw for people to participate.
  • When spending time with friends regularly, they usually do so for at least 2 hours.
  • The act of planning an activity (scavenger hunt or otherwise) tends to cause the most frustration in users.

Initial Designs

My early designs focused on merging quality content with a wealth of content, which would require both procedurally generated hunts (similar to Pokémon Go) and user-created hunts (similar to Actionbound). User created hunts would require purchasing to help encourage quality in the creations. To alleviate user frustration while planning the hunts, I designed a scheduling poll that would allow each invitee to select which days and times worked best for them, with minimal back and forth necessary.

User Testing & Revisions

Naming the App

Before beginning testing, I wanted to have a name to call the app. I decided upon TurfQuest to give it a sense of adventure

With this name, I renamed all scavenger hunts to "Quests" and was ready for testing!

Usability Testing

Armed with an early prototype, I conducted moderated remote usability testing with 6 participants to understand the strengths and weaknesses of the project so far.

The testing resulted in a few key takeaways:

  • Scheduling polls was the main source of tension and confusion for my users.
  • There were too few signifiers that custom quests required payment prior to the payment page.
  • People were apprehensive to pay for custom quests.
  • When explaining what a scheduling poll was for, few participants saw the value in the feature.

Revising the Design

With these test findings, I went back to my sitemap to determine what needed to be changed. Scheduling polls clearly needed a revamp and I needed to rethink custom quests a bit.

I decided that changing scheduling polls would be too much work for too little gain, so I removed the feature entirely.

Custom Quests were given higher priority which would allow better communication on pricing to users ahead of time.

To reduce the Custom Quest's barrier to entry, I added In-App Currency (temporarily called "TurfCoins"). TurfCoins could be used to purchase in-app rewards or Custom Quests. This would help remove the barrier to entry on creating custom quests while still allowing them to be a premium experience.

Lastly, I added "Side Quests," which are weekly challenges players could participate in to earn in-app currency. Side Quests would be generic enough that anyone would be able to complete them regardless of location, they would be able to be completed during users' regular weekly routines with some detours and would also be very easy to complete while participating in a Quest.

Side Quest Examples

  1. Walk X # of Steps
    • This is an always active, tiered goal. As players reach a milestone, they get a reward and a new goal
  2. Vist a park 2 times
  3. Meet up with another player
  4. Visit a Restaurant
